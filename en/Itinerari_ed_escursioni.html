Montefiascone

Located about 600 meters above sea level, Montefiascone overlooks Lake Bolsena from above, offering breathtaking views over the Tuscia area of Viterbo. From the highest point in the town, the Pilgrim’s Tower of the Rocca dei Papi, you can enjoy a panorama that stretches across the lake, the Viterbo valley, and, on particularly clear days, even a glimpse of the Tyrrhenian Sea. The town is famous for its EST! EST!! EST!!! wine, one of the region’s finest products, tied to the well-known legend of the German bishop Johannes Defuk, who stopped here on his journey to Rome, captivated by the quality of the local wine. During the summer, Montefiascone comes alive with numerous events connected to the Wine Fair and the Tuscia Wine Festivals, featuring tastings, shows, and cultural happenings such as the Est! Film Festival and the Baroque Festival. One of the most evocative highlights is the Historical Parade, with participants in medieval costumes reenacting the town’s history and traditions. Among the must-see landmarks are the Cathedral of Saint Margaret, with one of the largest domes in Italy, the Rocca dei Papi, a former medieval papal residence, and the Basilica of San Flaviano, closely linked to the tradition of the famous local wine.

Bolsena

About 15 km from Montefiascone lies Bolsena, a town known worldwide for the Eucharistic Miracle of Corpus Domini. The stones stained with the blood that is said to have flowed from a consecrated host in the 13th century can still be seen today in the Basilica of St. Christina, which dates back to the 11th century. The basilica is dedicated to Saint Christina, a martyr who was killed in the 3rd century AD. Every year on July 24, Bolsena celebrates Saint Christina by recounting her story through “living tableaux” in the traditional performance known as the Mysteries.

Marta

Marta, just 10 km from Montefiascone, also overlooks Lake Bolsena. Its historic center is characterized by tuff stone houses, a maze of narrow streets and stairways, and is dominated by the Clock Tower, which defines the town’s skyline. Every year on May 14, the Festa delle Passate (commonly known as the “Barabbata”) is celebrated, featuring an offering of gifts to the Virgin Mary by local residents, who proceed in a procession to the ancient Sanctuary of the Madonna del Monte.

Capodimonte

Capodimonte, with its stunning Rocca Farnese, is also well known for the influx of tourists during the summer months. In addition to its beaches, major attractions include the many local festivals and food fairs centered around regional products. From the port of Capodimonte, it is possible to purchase tickets for guided tours to the islands of Martana and Bisentina.

Civita di Bagnoregio

Civita di Bagnoregio, about 14 km from Montefiascone, overlooks the Valle dei Calanchi, a UNESCO World Heritage site. Over the centuries, the valley has been shaped by erosion caused by atmospheric agents, resulting in its distinctive and striking lunar-like landscape, reminiscent of the Grand Canyon in Colorado. Due to the erosion of the clay beneath the tuff plateau on which Civita stands, the town gradually lost its connection to the main roads. Today, the only way to reach it is on foot or by mule, crossing a long concrete pedestrian bridge and climbing a series of wide steps. As a result of these changes, the town has slowly been abandoned, with only a few dozen residents remaining. For this reason, Civita di Bagnoregio is also known as “the dying city.” The fortified historic center, with its partially demolished towers, the monumental entrance gate with its supporting buttresses, and the beautiful Romanesque church in the main square, represents a unique and well-preserved example of a medieval village. Walking through its narrow streets, visitors can still admire rows of medieval houses built from local tuff stone.

Viterbo

The city of Viterbo (the provincial capital), located 16 km from Montefiascone, is renowned for its rich medieval history. A clear testament to its ancient splendor is the historic San Pellegrino district, still perfectly preserved, along with the ancient walls that enclose the old town. Notable landmarks also include the Church of Santa Maria Nuova, the Church of Saint Andrew, and the Papal Palace. Viterbo is also known as the “City of the Popes,” as the Papal Palace hosted the papal court in the 13th century. Well worth a visit are the Etruscan necropolises of Norchia and Castel d’Asso, famous for their large tombs, some of which were built to hold up to thirty individuals. Every year on September 3, Viterbo celebrates its “young” saint, Saint Rose, who died at just eighteen years old and is deeply revered by the people of Viterbo for her many miracles. The celebrations of Saint Rose culminate on September 3 with the transport of the famous “Macchina”: a tower over thirty meters tall, carried on the shoulders of one hundred “facchini” (porters) through the streets of the historic center.

Bagnaia

Just four kilometers from Viterbo lies the village of Bagnaia, famous for Villa Lante, a splendid and perfectly preserved example of an Italian-style garden. Among the other towns in the province of Viterbo worth visiting are Caprarola, renowned for its Palazzo Farnese, and Bomarzo, known for its Park of the Monsters.

Tuscania

Tuscania is about twenty kilometers from Montefiascone. The town stands on seven tuff stone promontories overlooking the valley of the Marta River, an important communication route in Etruscan times that provided access to the sea. Numerous legends surround the founding of Tuscania; among them, one in particular claims that the town was founded by Ascanius, the son of Aeneas, on the site where twelve puppies were discovered—hence the name Tus-cana, the ancient Roman name of the city. Must-see attractions in Tuscania include the Romanesque churches of San Pietro and Santa Maria Maggiore, the Rivellino Castle, the Tower of Lavello, and the Cathedral.

Montalto di Castro

Located on the Tyrrhenian coast of the Tuscia, about a half-hour drive from Tuscania, Montalto di Castro is an ideal destination for those who want to combine sea, nature, and history. Its wide, well-equipped beaches offer services, beach resorts, and sports centers where visitors can enjoy activities such as sailing, fishing, and water sports. The surrounding area is rich in evidence of the ancient Etruscan civilization. Just a few kilometers from the town center lies Vulci, one of the most important Etruscan cities in the region, now a vast archaeological park set in nature. Here, visitors can admire the remains of the ancient city and explore the Archaeological Museum housed in the evocative Castello dell’Abbadia, distinguished by its striking medieval bridge spanning the Fiora River. With its blend of seaside charm, archaeology, and the landscapes of the Lazio Maremma, Montalto di Castro is a perfect destination for those who wish to discover the millennia-old history of the Tuscia while still enjoying the relaxation of the coast.

Tarquinia

An ancient fortified center of Etruscan and Roman origin, Tarquinia is one of the richest cities in the Tuscia in terms of history and culture. Located a short distance from the Tyrrhenian Sea, it offers not only beautiful beaches and facilities for water sports, but also an archaeological heritage of extraordinary importance. The city is world-renowned for its Etruscan necropolises, particularly the Monterozzi Necropolis, a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Here, numerous tombs carved into tuff rock are decorated with frescoes depicting scenes of daily life, rituals, and traditions of the ancient Etruscan civilization. In the historic center, visitors can also find the National Archaeological Museum of Tarquinia, housed in the magnificent Palazzo Vitelleschi. The museum preserves valuable funerary artifacts, including sarcophagi, sculptures, and the famous terracotta of the Winged Horses, once placed on the pediment of the temple on the Acropolis. Visiting Tarquinia means immersing yourself in a place where history, art, and landscape blend together, offering a fascinating journey into the heart of ancient Etruria.

Orvieto

Although not geographically located in the Lazio region (Orvieto lies in Umbria, in the province of Terni), its history is closely linked to that of the Tuscia area of Viterbo. The construction of the Cathedral, for example, is connected to the Miracle of Corpus Domini that took place in Bolsena in the 13th century. Moreover, in ancient times, Orvieto was one of the twelve Etruscan city-states, known as Velzna. Today, Orvieto attracts a significant number of visitors throughout the year, not only for its monuments—such as the Cathedral, the Church of San Giovenale, St. Patrick’s Well, the Necropolises of Crocifisso del Tufo and Cannicella, and the Temple of Belvedere—but also for the celebrations related to the Miracle of Corpus Domini and the Umbria Jazz music festival.

Caprarola

The village of Caprarola, set among the green hills of the Cimini Mountains, is one of the most charming destinations in northern Lazio. This small, elegant, and harmonious town stands out for its unique urban layout, which develops along a main uphill street lined with historic buildings and picturesque views. Overlooking the village from above rises the majestic Palazzo Farnese, an extraordinary Renaissance residence with a pentagonal plan. Built in the 16th century for the powerful Farnese family, the palace is a masterpiece of architecture and art: inside, visitors can admire frescoed halls, monumental staircases, and Italian-style gardens that reflect the grandeur and refinement of the era.
